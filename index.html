Iconic subway seen to outlast pandemic's lasting effects

Trains shunned, but not abandoned, depict reliance on New York's lifeblood

By Prinz Magtulis

July 28, 2022

When Noelle Morcos finally decided to work fully remote for Meta in August, she weighed the costs and benefits of doing so: on one hand, she'll lose access to office perks and the chance to bond with her teammates in person. On the other, she no longer needed to rush to the office, could walk her dog in between virtual meetings, and most especially avoid the subway.
Employees like Morcos have minimized riding the subway, whether because they got used working from home at the height of pandemic, or fear getting victimized amid rising crime (which also contributed to her decision). Regardless of reason, their decision has had serious repercussions on the train system: over a year since mobility restrictions were lifted, ridership has not gone up beyond 60% of their pre-pandemic level in 2019.
Yet even as riders stayed away, they never completely abandoned the subway. Morcos unhesitatingly boards the train on weekends, relying on its vast network to meet friends and run errands. "There's no better way to get to your destination fast and the subway is also less crowded during weekends so it's very convenient," she said.
The subway shuttles over 3.1 million passengers on average during weekdays, and nearly 2 million during weekends, according to data from the Metropolitan Transit Authority (MTA). Those are still way below pre-pandemic levels, but, as Yonah Freemark, senior research associate at Washington, D.C.-based Urban Institute puts it, "that's still a lot of people" and crowding in coaches is still very much common these days.
Crowding may speak of the many things ignored of the aging railway — inadequate federal and state aid plus huge budget deficits set to reach $4.8 billion this year meant underinvestment. But when examined using the lens of how New Yorkers depended on the subway to move around, the dire image carries a more positive meaning of an infrastructure that has become the lifeblood of the city.
Hence despite grim financial prospects, analysts are one in saying that the subway is here to stay. Ridership may decline, but people will continue to patronize it without a more viable transport alternative.
"The subway plays an incredibly important role in just assuring basic daily mobility for the majority of people," Freemark said.
The reliance on the subway is not due to inadequate transport options. On the ground, buses ply the roads, cabs hailed, and Uber rides are booked. Nearly half of the city's households also own a car. But these alternatives, while always available, get beaten by the subway for efficiency and punctuality.
Sure, the subway also gets delayed sometimes— MTA data showed that in May, passengers waited an additional 82 seconds than usual during peak hours on weekdays— but unless there is maintenance work or, God forbid, an accident happens, commuters can easily estimate how long their trip will be.
New York traffic is the worst among key cities in the U.S. The city is also so dense that there's no space to build new roads, highways or even parking lots to accommodate more vehicles, Freemark said. "That's what makes the subway a fundamental part of the city," he said.

The subway is not only reliable, but also affordable. Hanna Nicole Daanoy, 24, takes the F- or E-train from Queens to W 4th Street to her work at L' Artusi restaurant in West Village daily. Since she sends nearly a quarter of her monthly earnings to her family in the Philippines as allowance, she follows a very strict budget that barely accounts for anything more than an Uber ride.
Using Uber's own fare calculator, an Uber X ride going home from work would cost Daanoy roughly $20 at the minimum, nearly tenfold the subway fare. "I can't afford to take a cab or Uber every day. I won't be able to eat anymore," she said in jest.
While prices of several items from potatoes to real estate are increasing with inflation, MTA fares have been steady for nearly three years now. The management was supposed to hike fares by 4% in 2021 but decided against it to ease the burden of customers reeling from the pandemic.
It remains to be seen whether the fare increase would push through this year, but when the initial delay was announced, Patrick Foye, former MTA chair and chief executive, also cited the need to attract back riders, who, theoretically, would have been deterred by higher fares.
New normal for MTA
Since then, ridership improved a bit. Data showed average weekday passengers rose to 57% of their 2019 level on the third week of July, up from about 49% same period a year ago. Thomas Abdallah, deputy vice president and chief environmental engineer at the MTA, said with gas prices still elevated, car owners may "think twice" about paying more at the pump, ride the subway instead, and support ridership further.
But returning to pre-pandemic level may no longer be realistic. In October, the MTA projected a "new normal" of about 1.5 billion customers annually by 2024, down from around 1.7 billion before 2020, citing a "change on behavior" among commuters for the projected decline. Abdallah said remote work is to blame as it becomes entrenched even when the pandemic is over.
Office occupancy in New York has so far hovered around 40%, according to Kastle Systems, a consultancy that tracks entries to offices weekly. Our analysis showed that office occupancy tends to move proportionately with subway ridership. That greatly helps explain why even with pandemic restrictions lifted for over a year now, low passenger volumes have persisted.

The MTA embarked on several efforts to attract its old patrons back, including adding more train services, offering discounts, and a fare-cap mechanism. Under the latter, commuters using the OMNY fare system get unlimited free rides for the rest of the week after their 12th consecutive ride. The program has not been officially assessed, but Abdallah said the impact on ridership would have been limited at best.
"Most people take the train 10 times a week, going to the office and back, and that's during normal times," he said, indicating that there may only be a few people qualifying to the promo.
Still safe
Safety is another issue. There is growing perception that riding the subway is unsafe because of several stories of people getting assaulted, shot or pushed over the tracks. This, in turn, has scared off both old and new riders.
Mio Tomita arrived in June for a summer program and has been taking the subway from 96th Street station to Columbia. But that will change come mid-August when her mom and daughter join her in the city. “To be honest, a lot of my colleagues here tell me not to take the subway,” said Tomita, who works as a reporter in Japan. "If it's just me, it's okay. But my mom and daughter see the news from here and taking the subway make them scared," she said.
While safety concerns are valid, Danny Pearlstein, policy communications director at Riders Alliance, a commuter's group, stressed that the subway remains remarkably safe. Government data support this.

The city has also responded to safety concerns, increasing police presence in stations during peak hours. Mayor Eric Adams also pledged to augment response teams and open more "safe haven" centers to assist riders.
For the most part however, Freemark said commuters ride the subway because the convenience far outweigh the risks. And this shows even among people who have recently grown averse it: Morcos said she doesn't foresee ditching the subway anytime soon. Daanoy said it's just impossible to do it, while Tomita, the tourist, said it remains an option for her when visiting.
Attracting passengers lost back to the system is good, but Abdallah said it also may be time to shift focus to the ones left. Planning for a "new normal", he said, involves devising a plan that involves the efficient use of subway resources. “I don't know the exact answer,” he said, but added that assessing whether current peak and non-peak hours are still accurate, given remote work, is a good starting point.
Pearlstein, meanwhile, is advocating for more state and federal aid to offset losses from fare revenues. The latter account for 28% of MTA's annual operating budget so if ridership, as MTA projects, will now be lower than pre-pandemic normal, money used to sustain operations should come from somewhere else. The last federal aid was given last year as part of Covid-19 response, but transport advocates are calling for more.
Against other metrics, subway ridership has lagged behind retail sales, job generation, and Broadway ticket sales when it comes to pandemic recovery. In-person classes have long resumed, and tourists are flying back, yet none of them successfully ramped up ridership to a level the MTA would want it to be.
While the jury is still out as to how the subway's recovery will look like, one thing for sure is it will never be left deserted. "I don't think it's going to decline further," Freemark said, pertaining to ridership. And if Robert Paaswell, engineering professor at City University of New York, is to be believed, a full rebound may even be possible.
"People want to be in crowds, want to be with their office mates or go to the restaurant or bars," he said.
"People are in New York because they want to experience New York and the best way to do that is to ride the subway," Paaswell said.
